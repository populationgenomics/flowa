## Abstract

The F508 variant of the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance
regulator (CFTR) causes the most common form of cystic fibrosis. We
review the cellular consequences of the deletion and the quality-control
machinery that disposes of the misfolded protein.

## Background

CFTR is an anion channel in the apical membrane of secretory epithelia.
Folding occurs co-translationally at the endoplasmic reticulum, with
nucleotide-binding domain 1 (NBD1) and the membrane-spanning domain
contributing to the early folding intermediates.

## Mechanism

F508del leads to misfolded CFTR retained in the endoplasmic reticulum.
NBD1 fails to acquire its native conformation, the molecule does not
satisfy ER quality control, and trafficking to the cell surface is
blocked. ER-associated degradation removes misfolded F508del before
reaching the cell surface, with most newly synthesised molecules
ubiquitinated and routed to the proteasome within minutes.

A small fraction of F508del CFTR can escape ER quality control under
some experimental conditions, but the protein that does reach the cell
surface exhibits reduced channel open probability and accelerated
turnover compared to wild-type.

## Implications

The molecular phenotype — misfolding plus accelerated degradation —
explains why F508del homozygotes show severe disease and why corrector
small molecules that stabilise NBD1 can partially restore surface
expression.
